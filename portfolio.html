Ackermann Steering System

Team

Tools

Status
Manufactured · On vehicle

The Problem

When a car turns, the inner and outer wheels trace arcs of different radii. If both wheels point at the same angle — parallel steering — the outer tire scrubs sideways, fighting the turn. Ackermann geometry solves this by angling the inner wheel more sharply than the outer, so both wheels steer toward a common turning center.

For Triton Solar Car, the challenge wasn't just choosing Ackermann geometry — it was choosing how much. Full Ackermann minimizes tire scrub at low speeds. Partial Ackermann distributes lateral load more evenly at higher speeds. A solar race car operates across both regimes. The task: run a full trade study and design a linkage that found the right balance.

The Geometry Work

The starting point was physical measurement — locating exactly where the kingpins sat and where the rack and pinion lived on the car. From there, I drew construction lines in SolidWorks from the rear axle centerpoint through each kingpin axis. The key insight from research: the tie rod endpoints have to fall somewhere on those construction lines for the geometry to produce true Ackermann behavior. That gave me a constraint to work within rather than a blank canvas.

From there it was iteration. I tested different tie rod pickup positions along those lines, each one producing a different Ackermann percentage, and evaluated the tradeoff between scrub elimination at low speed and lateral load distribution at higher speed. The final geometry — 21.5° inner / 16.8° outer — was the point where scrubbing was effectively eliminated without overcorrecting into full Ackermann, which would have loaded the outer tire too aggressively in faster corners.

The Kingpin Axis Problem

When the suspension team's design came in, the kingpin geometry introduced a complication — the axis wasn't rotating purely about the Y-axis as a simplified model would assume. The actual physical setup meant the rotation had a Z-component too, which changes how you think about the load cases going through that joint.

The honest answer is this is a first-generation car. No one on the team had built one before. The call I made was to assume a simplified straight axis for the analysis and proceed — because one of the things you learn quickly in engineering is that you can't calculate everything to the T. Sometimes the right move is to make a defensible assumption, document it, build the part, and see what the real world tells you. The design is on the car now. That's the test that matters.

A perfect model of a flawed input is still a flawed output. We made a reasonable assumption, built the part, and committed to learning from the physical result — which is how first-generation engineering actually works.

FEA Validation

To validate the design before cutting steel, the team ran static structural analysis in ANSYS under worst-case combined front kingpin loading — simulating a hard turn combined with an impact event, which maximizes stress through the linkage simultaneously. I worked through interpreting the outputs and understanding what the stress distribution meant for our geometry and material choice.

Fabrication

Fabrication started with CNC wood prototypes — a fast, cheap way to validate the geometry before committing to steel. Once the linkage positions checked out on the car, the parts were recut in steel plate and assembled. Getting the tie rod endpoints to land in exactly the right positions was the critical tolerance — small deviations there change the effective Ackermann percentage, so fitting was done carefully against the SolidWorks reference geometry.

Steering Wheel Control Module

Alongside the steering geometry, I'm designing the ergonomics side — a button module backplate that houses horn, turn signals, lighting, and driver comms. The constraint is ASC regulations require all controls to rotate with the wheel, which means the housing needs to clip onto the button module and turn with the column rather than being fixed to the chassis.

The hardest part was spatial. The button module had an unusual elliptical profile with awkward dimensions, so I modelled it fully in CAD before even touching the assembly — getting every curve and hole position exact so the housing would actually be snug rather than floating around inside the wheel. From there it was a positioning problem: figuring out the right standoff distance to sit the module close to the driver without fouling the wheel structure, which meant live iteration in the assembly moving parts around until the clearances felt right.

I've 3D printed several iterations. The typical failure mode is either the mounting holes coming out slightly oversized from print shrinkage, or the curved face not quite matching the mold contour. Each print tells me something specific to fix — it's a tighter feedback loop than refining a model in isolation.

A Lesson in Simplicity

My first CAD design for the backplate holder was overengineered. I added features, brackets, and geometry that solved problems I hadn't actually encountered yet — designing defensively against hypothetical failure modes instead of building for what the part actually needed to do.

One of the most valuable engineering lessons: never make something more complicated than it needs to be. Complexity is a liability, not a signal of effort.

Overview

Center for Community Energy is a San Diego-based nonprofit focused on accelerating distributed energy adoption for commercial and institutional clients. As an engineering intern, my role is genuinely broad — I do a bit of everything. On any given week that means policy research, financial modeling (NPV, ROI, ITC analysis), BOM development, structural feasibility consulting with external parties, and hands-on system design in Helioscope. The work sits at the intersection of engineering and energy policy, which is exactly where the most interesting problems live.

What the Work Actually Looks Like

A typical project starts with utility bill analysis — pulling interval data to understand a client's load profile, peak demand timing, and blended rate structure. From there, solar production is modeled in Helioscope: laying out the array, configuring electrical stringing, and running shading analysis to get a credible energy output estimate.

Utility Bill Analysis

Understanding the baseline is what makes a financial model defensible. We pull interval billing data to map out annual usage, peak demand, and the blended rate structure — this is what the solar system needs to offset.

Load Profile & Solar Production Modeling

Load profile modeling overlays solar production against actual facility demand throughout the day and across seasons. Dual-axis trackers follow the sun, maximizing the overlap between peak solar output and peak building load — which is where the real bill savings come from.

Financial Modeling

The production model feeds into a 20-year financial analysis: NPV, payback period, ITC and MACRS depreciation, O&M escalation — built to be rigorous enough to put in front of a client's accountant. Safe-harbor provisions and federal incentive timing are factored into the acquisition strategy.

On the engineering side, I coordinate with panel manufacturers on electrical and mechanical compatibility and reach out to structural engineers to verify carport load capacity. The policy side — incentive structures, zoning, permitting, municipal outreach — is just as much a part of the workflow as the technical modeling.

03 — Humanitarian Solar

Solar PV Carport for the Shatila Refugee Camp

Published
Curieux Academic Journal, Pg. 278
Location

Tools

The Problem

The Shatila refugee camp in Beirut, Lebanon was established in 1949 and now houses an estimated 40,000 residents in severely overcrowded conditions. One of the most pressing daily problems: residents receive only 1–3 hours of government electricity per day. The 2020 Beirut explosion compounded this — fuel prices surged, and residents were forced to pay privately owned generators within the camp for electricity at increasingly unaffordable rates.

The core research question: could a solar PV carport installed in the nearby Camille Chamoun Sports City Stadium parking lot supply meaningful energy to the camp? Installing panels within the camp itself was not viable — the density of the housing, safety concerns, and lack of flat available space ruled it out. The stadium parking lot, a large flat site with high solar irradiance and no obstructions, was the only realistic option.

Site Analysis

Using Google Earth Pro and the Global Solar Atlas, I characterized the site: GHI of 1,904.8 kWh/m², DNI of 1,968.3 kWh/m², and an optimum panel tilt of 27° facing south. The sunpath analysis confirmed the location receives strong solar access year-round with no significant terrain shading.

First Iteration — Area Estimation

Before designing the actual layout, I ran five estimates using 60–100% of the parking lot area to get a range of output figures accounting for roads, gaps, and infrastructure. This gave a defensible range before committing to detailed CAD work.

Design Methodology

I ran two iterations. The first used percentage-based area calculations — modeling what the farm would produce if 60%, 70%, 80%, 90%, and 100% of the parking lot area was used. This gave a range of output estimates accounting for roads, spacing, and infrastructure.

The second and more accurate iteration involved designing the actual carport layout in AutoCAD — placing individual T-configuration carport rows with proper 2.7m car lanes between them, then counting every panel that physically fit. Panel specification: Jinko Tiger Pro 72HC at 550W each.

Results

The AutoCAD iteration fit 4,736 panels — a 2,604.8 kWp system producing an estimated 4.383 GWh per year. Against the camp's estimated annual demand of 31,260,000 kWh, the farm would supply 14.02% of total energy needs.

Conclusion

14% is not a complete solution — but in a camp where residents currently get 1–3 hours of power a day, a system that meaningfully expands that is worth building. The study also identified the two main barriers: the estimated $2.5M construction cost and the need for Lebanese government approval to build on the stadium site.

Engineering research doesn't always end with a solved problem. Sometimes the value is in precisely defining what's possible, what it costs, and what stands in the way.

Study 1 — Wind Turbine Rotor Swept Area

The question driving this study: does wind turbine power output scale predictably with rotor swept area? The Betz limit — the theoretical maximum efficiency of any wind turbine — says power should scale with the square of rotor radius, but real-world behavior deviates, especially at low wind speeds where flow separation and tip vortices complicate things.

To test this, I designed and 3D-printed a series of variable-radius rotor blades, each geometrically identical except for length. A controlled fan provided consistent wind input while I measured voltage and current output across each rotor size. The R² = 0.794 correlation between rotor radius and electrical power output confirmed the general swept-area scaling relationship while revealing nonlinear behavior at lower wind speeds — experimental results that matched trends reported in existing literature.

Study 2 — Optimal Solar Panel Tilt Angle

Solar panels mounted at a fixed angle sacrifice energy in every month where the sun's position deviates from that angle. This study applied established solar geometry models to calculate the optimal tilt angle for each calendar month at a specific site latitude — a more practical output than a single annual average.

The method used calculus-based optimization: deriving the beam irradiation ratio R̄b as a function of panel tilt angle β, then differentiating with respect to β and solving for the angle that maximizes monthly irradiance capture. The formula accounts for solar declination, hour angle, and site latitude — all varying by month. Results produced a complete monthly tilt schedule validated against known solar positioning models.
